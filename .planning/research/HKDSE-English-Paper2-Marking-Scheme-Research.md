## 1. Assessment Framework

| Component | Weighting | Duration | Task | Suggested Length |
|-----------|-----------|----------|------|-----------------|
| **Part A — Guided Writing** | 10% of total subject | 2 hrs total | Short practical task (email, letter, announcement, etc.) | ~200 words |
| **Part B — Extended Writing** | 15% of total subject | (shared with Part A) | Choose 1 from 8 questions; long-form (article, essay, blog, proposal, speech, etc.) | ~400 words |

- Part A is **more restrictive** — less room for candidates to demonstrate range, but also less forgiving of errors.
- Part B is **more open-ended** — allows top candidates to differentiate through creativity, sophisticated language, and structure.
- Recommended time allocation: **40 min Part A + 80 min Part B**.

Both parts are marked out of **21 marks** (7 Content + 7 Language + 7 Organization). Each script receives **four scores**: two markers × two parts.

## 2. The 7-Point Scoring Grid (C-L-O)

This is the **detailed marking grid** used by examiners (from the HKEAA Standards-referenced Reporting Information Package). The grid below applies to **both** Part A and Part B, though markers may supplement task-specific guidance.

> **Important:** If content is off-topic, candidates **should not be awarded more than 3 marks** in each of the other domains (Language, Organization).

### Content (C) — 7 marks

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| **7** | Content is **very extensive and often exceeds the requirements** of the question. Totally relevant. **All ideas are well developed.** Creativity and imagination are **consistently** shown. Engages the reader's interest **skilfully** and shows a **fine-tuned awareness of audience**. |
| **6** | Content is **extensive and entirely fulfils** the requirements of the question. Totally relevant. **Most ideas are well developed.** Creativity and imagination are shown **when appropriate**. Engages the reader's interest **consistently** and shows a **high awareness of audience**. |
| **5** | Content is **extensive and fulfils** the requirements of the question. Totally relevant. **Main ideas are well developed.** Creativity and imagination are shown when appropriate. Maintains the reader's interest and shows **general awareness of audience** throughout. |
| **4** | Content is **relevant and addresses** the requirements of the question. Some ideas are developed. Some evidence of creativity and imagination. Engages the reader's interest in parts. |
| **3** | Content is **mostly relevant** but may not fully address all requirements. Some main points are present but development is limited. Occasional evidence of creativity. |
| **2** | **Some relevant content** but many points are undeveloped or tangentially related. Limited awareness of task requirements. |
| **1** | **Little relevant content.** Few points address the task. Minimal or no development. |
| **0** | Completely off-task / no attempt. |

### Language (L) — 7 marks

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| **7** | **Unlimited range** of accurate sentence structures, with an **excellent grasp** of more complex structures. Grammar **extremely accurate**. Vocabulary **well-chosen** and used appropriately to express **subtleties of meaning**. Spelling and punctuation are **entirely correct**. Register, tone and style are used to **deliberate effect** and are **entirely appropriate** to the genre and text type. |
| **6** | **Very wide range** of accurate sentence structures, with a **good grasp** of more complex structures. Grammar accurate with only **very minor slips**. Vocabulary well-chosen and often used appropriately to express subtleties of meaning. Spelling and punctuation **almost entirely correct**. Register, tone and style are **entirely appropriate** to the genre and text type. |
| **5** | **Wide range** of accurate sentence structures with a good grasp of simple and compound sentences. Grammar **mainly accurate** with occasional common errors that **do not affect overall clarity**. Vocabulary is wide, with **many examples of more sophisticated lexis**. Spelling and punctuation **mostly correct**. Register, tone and style are **appropriate** to the genre and text type. |
| **4** | **Range of sentence structures** used accurately and appropriately. Punctuation and grammar is **sufficiently accurate to convey meaning**. Errors do not affect overall clarity. Vocabulary is **moderately wide**, appropriate, and most words are spelt correctly. Register, tone and style are **mostly appropriate**. |
| **3** | **Simple sentences and some complex sentences** are well formed. Basic punctuation and some basic grammatical structures are accurate. **Common vocabulary** used appropriately and spelt correctly. **Some evidence** of use of register, tone and style appropriate to the text type. |
| **2** | **Simple sentences** are well formed. **Most basic punctuation** is used correctly. Grammatical accuracy is sufficient to make **some sentences comprehensible**. Simple vocabulary is appropriate and most is spelt correctly. |
| **1** | **A few simple, comprehensible sentences.** A few examples of simple vocabulary used appropriately. |
| **0** | Language too limited to assess / no attempt. |

### Organization (O) — 7 marks

| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| **7** | Paragraphs are organized **extremely effectively**, with **logical development of ideas from topic sentences** and **all points supported by relevant details**. Cohesive ties between and within paragraphs are **sophisticated**. Overall structure is **coherent, extremely sophisticated** and entirely appropriate to the genre and text type. |
| **6** | Paragraphs are organized **effectively**, with logical development of ideas from topic sentences, and **most points supported by relevant details**. **Strong cohesive ties** between and within paragraphs. Overall structure is **coherent, sophisticated** and appropriate to the genre and text type. |
| **5** | Paragraphs are **mostly organized effectively**, with logical development of ideas from topic sentences, and **main points supported by relevant details**. **Sound cohesive ties** between and within paragraphs. Overall structure is **coherent** and appropriate to the genre and text type. |
| **4** | Structure is **coherent in most parts** and appropriate to the genre and text type. Paragraphing is **sufficiently effective** for overall coherence to be maintained. Cohesion between **most sentences and paragraphs** is successful. |
| **3** | **Some sections** are coherent and appropriate. Paragraphing is effective **in parts**. Cohesion between **some** sentences and paragraphs is successful. |
| **2** | Structure **can be discerned** when genre and text type is simple and familiar. **Some evidence** of paragraphing. **Some simple links** between sentences and paragraphs. |
| **1** | **A few links** made between sentences. |
| **0** | No discernible organization / no attempt. |

## 3. Official Level Descriptors (Public Document)

The HKEAA publishes **Level Descriptors** for the Writing paper. These are broader than the 7-point grid and describe the **typical performance** at each reported level.

### Level 5
| Domain | Descriptor |
|--------|-----------|
| **Content** | The content is relevant and extensive, shows an awareness of purpose, and engages the reader's interest. Creativity and imagination are shown when appropriate. |
| **Language & Style** | A wide range of sentence structures is used accurately and appropriately. Meaning is conveyed through accurate and appropriate punctuation and grammar. Vocabulary is wide and appropriate, with some use of more ambitious and sophisticated language. Register, tone and style are appropriate to the text type. |
| **Organization** | The structure of the writing is wholly coherent and appropriate to the genre and text type. Paragraphing is effective. Cohesion between sentences and paragraphs is sophisticated. |

### Level 4
| Domain | Descriptor |
|--------|-----------|
| **Content** | The content is relevant, in parts detailed and engages the reader's interest. Creativity and imagination are shown in most parts of the writing. |
| **Language & Style** | A range of sentence structures is used accurately and appropriately. Punctuation and grammar is sufficiently accurate to convey meaning. Errors do not affect overall clarity. Vocabulary is moderately wide, appropriate, and most words are spelt correctly. Register, tone and style are mostly appropriate to the text type. |
| **Organization** | The structure of the writing is coherent in most parts and appropriate to the genre and text type. Paragraphing is sufficiently effective for overall coherence to be maintained. Cohesion between most sentences and paragraphs is successful. |

### Level 3
| Domain | Descriptor |
|--------|-----------|
| **Content** | Most of the content is relevant. Several examples of creativity and imagination are evident. |
| **Language & Style** | Simple sentences, and some complex sentences are well formed. Basic punctuation and some basic grammatical structures are accurate. Common vocabulary is used appropriately and spelt correctly. Some evidence of use of register, tone and style appropriate to the text type. |
| **Organization** | Some sections of the writing are coherent and appropriate. Paragraphing is effective in parts. Cohesion between some sentences and paragraphs is successful. |

### Level 2
| Domain | Descriptor |
|--------|-----------|
| **Content** | There are some examples of relevant content. Some features of familiar genres are used. |
| **Language & Style** | Simple sentences are well formed. Most basic punctuation is used correctly. Grammatical accuracy is sufficient to make some sentences comprehensible. Simple vocabulary is appropriate and most is spelt correctly. |
| **Organization** | The structure of the writing can be discerned when the genre and text type is simple and familiar. Some evidence of paragraphing. Some simple links between sentences and paragraphs. |

### Level 1
| Domain | Descriptor |
|--------|-----------|
| **Content** | A few content points are relevant. |
| **Language** | There are a few simple, comprehensible sentences. A few examples of simple vocabulary used appropriately. |
| **Organization** | There are a few links made between sentences. |

## 4. Task Fulfillment — Defined

Task fulfillment is the **foundation** of the Content mark. It means the candidate has:

1. **Answered the question as asked** — addressed every explicit instruction
2. **Covered all required content points** — including directive verbs (describe, explain, give reasons, suggest, recommend)
3. **Adopted the correct text type** — article format, letter format, proposal structure, etc.
4. **Used the appropriate register** — formal for a letter to the editor, conversational for a blog, persuasive for a proposal
5. **Written for the specified audience** — addressed the reader correctly
6. **Followed administrative instructions** — used provided names, no real names, correct question number box

**When task fulfillment fails:**
- Misreading keywords (e.g., 2025: "vegetarian" → "vegetables" → wrote about farming → off-topic)
- Ignoring the required number of points (e.g., "give three reasons" but only giving two)
- Wrong format (e.g., writing an essay when a proposal is required)
- Wrong register (e.g., aggressive tone in a complaint email to a manager)
- Significantly under word count → "inevitably fall short of fulfilling the task requirements" (HKEAA FAQ)
- Off-topic content caps other domains at **max 3 marks** each

## 6. Examiner Report Findings (Common Weaknesses & Strengths)

### From HKEAA's Published Comments on Candidates' Performance

#### Common Weaknesses (all years)

| Weakness | Evidence from Examiner Reports |
|----------|-------------------------------|
| **Misreading the prompt** | 2025: "vegetarian" misinterpreted as "vegetables" → off-topic farming compositions |
| **Wrong register / tone** | Complaint emails that are "simply rude or so abrupt that it would have caused offence" (HKEAA practice paper report) |
| **Template overuse** | "Using robotic, memorized introductory phrases that do not fit the organic context" (2024 analysis); "I agree with you" default in speaking (2025) |
| **Lack of development** | "Simply repeating factual information" rather than developing ideas; listing points without elaboration |
| **Poor time management** | Significantly under word count OR writing too much for Part A leaving insufficient time for Part B |
| **Inconsistency** | Aggressive in complaint letters; dry academic prose in blog posts that should be engaging |
| **Format errors** | Not using correct text-type conventions (missing title for article, no date/address for letter) |
| **Basic grammar errors** | "Hong Kong have" instead of "Hong Kong has"; "people is" instead of "people are" |
| **Ignoring visual prompts** | Writing a generic response without referencing specific discrepancies shown in the prompt |

#### Common Strengths (in Level 5 scripts)

| Strength | Evidence from Level 5 Exemplar Comments |
|----------|----------------------------------------|
| **Audience awareness** | "Friendly and inviting tone" appropriate to text type; addressing readers directly |
| **Descriptive language** | "Scrumptious snacks", "brand-new 45-minute special", "award-winning photography" — vivid adjectives |
| **Rhetorical devices** | Hyperbole (comparing teacher football match to Ronaldo vs Messi), witty titles ("So THANKFUL for this Challenge") |
| **Register control** | "Low register, effortless tone and conversational style" for blog; formal for proposals |
| **Sophisticated cohesion** | "Shorter paragraphs signal the changing thoughts and behaviours"; cohesive ties between paragraphs |
| **Engaging openings** | Orients reader effectively to topic and stance in introductory paragraph |
| **Pronoun use** | "We need you", "you can grab the chance" — compelling and persuasive |
| **Collocations** | "Long awaited day", "professional footballers" — natural, accurate lexical choices |

### Specific Excerpts from Level 5 Exemplar Comments (HKEAA 2021)

> *"The writer's tone in Part A is very friendly and inviting, keeping the reader's interest engaged. He/she introduces him/herself as the President of the Students' Association and shows an awareness of purpose by providing the key information about the event at the start."*

> *"Creativity and imagination are shown through the use of descriptive and vivid adjectives, e.g. 'scrumptious snacks', 'brand-new 45-minute special', 'award-winning photography and paintings', 'student-in-charge game stalls' etc."*

> *"The writer starts with a title, 'So THANKFUL for this Challenge' which is a witty play on taking part in a gratitude challenge."*

> *"Shorter paragraphs signal the changing thoughts and behaviours over the 21 days and cohesion between sentences and paragraphs is sophisticated."*

> *"The low register, effortless tone and conversational style of writing are entirely appropriate to the text type. For example, the writer ends his/her blog by addressing his or her readers 'Good luck and stay awesome!'"*

## 7. Question Discrimination — What Makes a Good DSE Writing Question

A good DSE writing question **discriminates** between ability levels. Research and analysis of past papers reveals these characteristics:

### Discriminating Questions (separate high from low performers)

| Feature | Why It Discriminates |
|---------|---------------------|
| **Clear task with specific requirements** | Low performers can address the basics; high performers can exceed them. E.g., "Give three reasons" — weak candidates list three; strong candidates develop, exemplify, and connect them. |
| **Demands genre awareness** | Forces candidates to show understanding of format, register, tone. A blog, a proposal, a letter to the editor, and a speech all have different conventions. |
| **Requires point of view / stance** | Argumentative tasks force candidates to take and defend a position rather than just describe. |
| **Open enough for creativity** | Allows top candidates to demonstrate imagination, wit, and sophisticated language. |
| **Specific enough to block pre-prepared answers** | Questions grounded in specific scenarios (e.g., "Your school has been awarded a government fund...") prevent generic essay memorization. |
| **Connects to the elective modules** | Questions are based on the 8 elective modules (Social Issues, Workplace Communication, Popular Culture, etc.) — depth of engagement reveals breadth of reading. |
| **Visual or data stimulus** | Requires candidates to integrate information from a stimulus rather than writing from general knowledge alone. |

### Non-Discriminating Questions (flatten performance)

| Feature | Why It Fails |
|---------|-------------|
| **Too generic** | "Write about a hobby" — everyone can produce something, no differentiation. |
| **Overly prescriptive** | Every single content point specified — no room for top candidates to exceed. |
| **Niche / inaccessible** | Topic too unfamiliar or culturally specific → low performers can't engage, top performers also struggle. |
| **Format-neutral** | If no clear genre is signaled, candidates default to a generic "essay" with no format awareness test. |

## 8. Marking Process & Key Rules

### How Scripts Are Marked
1. Each script is marked by **two independent markers**.
2. Each marker gives **three scores per task** (C / L / O out of 7).
3. Each candidate receives **4 sets of scores**: Part A (Marker 1), Part A (Marker 2), Part B (Marker 1), Part B (Marker 2).
4. These 4 scores are combined with weighting (Part A = 10%, Part B = 15%) to produce a paper total.
5. **Grading** (converting total score to Level 1–5**) is a separate process done by expert judges who set cut scores each year.

### "Best Fit" Principle (from HKEAA FAQ)
> *"Markers are instructed to make a judgment based on which of the levels in each domain is the 'best fit' for the candidate's performance. Sometimes a candidate's work may be stronger in one of the subcategories than the others. Markers are instructed to decide what level on balance best describes the script."*

### Off-Topic Penalty
> *"If content is off-topic, candidates should not be awarded more than 3 marks in each of the other domains."*

This means a completely off-topic script can receive at most 3/7 for Language and 3/7 for Organization — i.e., a maximum possible score of 9/21 per task even with perfect language use.

## 9. Implications for AI-Based Writing Evaluation & Generation

### For Scoring / Evaluation

The DSE marking scheme requires AI evaluation to:

1. **Separate C / L / O scores** — do not collapse into a single holistic score
2. **Check task fulfillment first** — if content is off-topic, cap L and O at 3/7
3. **Detect template use** — penalize robotic, memorized phrases (a known examiner concern)
4. **Evaluate register control** — verify tone/style matches text type (blog ≠ letter ≠ proposal)
5. **Reward creativity** — identify vivid adjectives, rhetorical devices, engaging openings
6. **Detect underdevelopment** — listing without elaboration should not score above 4/7 for Content
7. **Apply "best fit"** — mixed performance within a domain uses holistic judgment, not average of sub-skills

### For Prompt Generation

1. **Specificity is key** — prompts must have clear role, audience, format, and task requirements
2. **Include constraints** — "give three reasons", "suggest two ways", "describe one experience"
3. **Provide context** — scenario-based rather than abstract topics
4. **Signal genre** — the text type must be unmistakable (article? proposal? blog? letter?)
5. **Leave room for creativity** — the best prompts have a clear framework within which top candidates can excel
6. **Avoid cultural inaccessibility** — topics must be meaningful to Hong Kong secondary students
7. **Reflect elective modules** — meaningfully connect to one of the 8 modules (Social Issues, Workplace Communication, Popular Culture, Short Stories, Poems & Songs, Sports Communication, Debating, Drama)
